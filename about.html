My Tumor Story

I don't like to share this - ask my friends and family, I am a very private person. Hell, many of my friends I have made sense my diagnosis don't even know about it. But, and "About Me" section would be pretty bullshit without it. So, here it goes.

In November of 2021, I was diagnosed with a Giant Cell Tumor in my right leg. I had been experiencing some serious pain in my right knee for several months - but having a history of surgery on my right knee, I chalked it up to reagrravating an old injury. Even after realizing it must be something more severe, my assumption was that the worst it would be was a torn ligament, after all the pain began while I was training for a marathon, I figured that was a reasonable explination for how it got hurt. I was also in my first semester of my freshman year of college, and I was not about to let knee pain keep me down, I knew people that delayed ACL surgery for years, what does it matter that my knee would randomly give out, that I had a slight limp, that I couldn't run, that if something touched my leg near the knee I would practically scream in pain.

I was being stupid. Extremely stupid.

Finally, in November 2022 I went to my orthopedic surgeon, who handled my previous knee surgery, and he ordered an MRI. I went to the MRI, and the very next day, I got a call from my surgeon asking me to come in as soon as possible that day. The appointment had been scheduled for the next week, but he wanted me in that day. That was the first sign that shit was bad. I remember sitting in the room, on one of those tables doctors have, and seeing the MRI on the screen. I have no clue how to read an MRI, I have looked at them before and never been able to decern shit. Well, even I could see the massive discolored lump, that my doctor would reveal was the size of a softball and literally eating out of the side of my femur.

The next couple weeks the C word was my entire families mind. In fact one orthopedic oncologist I went to straight up told me that he thought it was an Osteosarcom, and told me and my family to prepare for that. I Thank god I ended up getting into see my current orthopedic oncologist at the University of Michigan, who talked down that stress a little bit, and after doing a biopsy revealed it was actually a one-in-a-million Giant Cell Tumor of the Bone. Hearing the word "benign" after waiting several days for a biopsy results call is probably the most hardest I have happy cried in my life. After having an area the size of a softball scrapped out of my femur and reconstructed with bone cement, and the addition of a plate and 9 screws to hold it all together, I was momentarily tumor free.

But, the story doesn't end there. I was tumor free for about 6 months, before scans revealed that my tumor had came back. However, this time it was caught early, a similar size to a peanut. I was told that surgery was not an option at that time, and I would be put on a medication that proved useful in inhibiting the tumors growth. As I am writting this I still receive this medication as a shot every month, which has some minor side effects, but is overall not too bad, and the tumor presumably (knock on wood) doing well.

The reason I want to share this story is that I have wanted to be a resourse for people, specifically previously young, healthy college students that have suddenly had to deal with chronic health issues. I really do not like opening up about this to people, but if I felt supper alone especially when dealing with the cancer scare part of it and the realization that my leg will never be the same, and I want to be a resource for people that are going through similar things. So, if you are reading this and you are going through a similar experience, please feel free to reach out to me, I would love to talk to you. Even if you don't want to reach out and are going through a similar experience, I hope this story can help you feel less alone. Also, this is not all gloom, I did run my first 5k in 2 years last month, and have been scalling up running throuhout the last month. I am also planning on running a 10k next month. Am I as fast as before? Hell no. But I have found a new normal, and I hope others can as well.

So yeah, that is my tumor story. I hope it helps someone out there. Big shout out to my family, and friends for helping me through this, and to my doctors for saving my leg. Also shout out to the Hank Green, one of the Youtube Creators behind Crash Course and so many other channels I grew up on that currently opened up to the entire internet about having cancer, his sharing is what inspired me to share my story with my tromatic health experience.
